Allen Cyber Invitational

Local Cybersecurity Competition

Summary

The Allen Cyber Invitational is a student-created annual cybersecurity competition that is open to all high school students in the DFW area. Students will be challenged in many facets of cybersecurity such as cryptography, exploitation (Binary & Web) , shell commands, and system hardening. Teams will first participate in a seeding round where they will need to complete a series of CTF-based challenges. Based on each teams performance in the seeding round, they will be divided into three divisions (Platinum, Gold, and Silver). After divisions are made, teams will be presented with a collection of puzzles to complete to earn points for their team. After 40 minutes, teams will have to participate in a battle round, where they will wager points for a big a payout if they complete a difficult challenge. This will repeat 3 more times. Each division will have breaks during certain puzzle and battle rounds. Then students will have lunch and listen to a speaker. Awards will then be given to the winning teams in each division.

Insertion Sort

Speed
Medium
  • Worst-Case Time Complexity: \(n^2\)
  • Best-Case Time Complexity: \(n\)
  • Space Complexity: \(O(1)\)
  • Creator: John Mauchly
  • Made in: 1945

Difficulty
Easy

Insertion Sort is a simple sorting algorithm that builds the final sorted array one item at a time. It iterates through the input list, removing one element at a time and placing it at the correct position within the sorted portion of the list. This process continues until the entire list is sorted. Developed by computer scientist John Mauchly in 1945, Insertion Sort is known for its simplicity and efficiency on small datasets or partially sorted arrays.

The creator of Insertion Sort, John Mauchly, was a significant figure in the early days of computer science. He is perhaps best known for his co-invention of the Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer (ENIAC), one of the earliest general-purpose electronic digital computers. Insertion Sort is particularly useful for small lists or nearly sorted datasets, where its simplicity and adaptive nature allow for efficient sorting. However, its average and worst-case time complexity of O(n^2) makes it less suitable for larger datasets compared to more advanced algorithms like QuickSort or MergeSort, which have better average-case performances.

In comparison to other sorting algorithms, Insertion Sort holds its own in certain scenarios. It performs well on small datasets or when elements are already partially ordered. Unlike algorithms with higher time complexities, Insertion Sort can be more efficient in these specific cases. However, for larger datasets, more advanced algorithms with better average-case time complexities, such as QuickSort and MergeSort, are generally preferred in professional software development due to their superior performance in a broader range of scenarios.

Merge Sort

Speed
Fast
  • Worst-Case Time Complexity: \(n * log(n)\)
  • Best-Case Time Complexity: \(n * log(n))
  • Space Complexity: \(O(n)\)
  • Creator: John von Neumann
  • Made in: 1945

Difficulty
Moderate

Merge Sort is an efficient and popular sorting algorithm that follows the divide-and-conquer paradigm. It was developed by John von Neumann in 1945, although its detailed description was later published by John von Neumann and Herman Goldstine in 1951. Merge Sort operates by dividing the unsorted list into n sublists, each containing one element, and then repeatedly merging these sublists until a single sorted list is achieved. Its key strength lies in its consistent time complexity of O(n log n), making it more efficient than many other sorting algorithms for large datasets.

John von Neumann, a brilliant mathematician and computer scientist, is widely recognized for his foundational contributions to various fields, including computer science. Merge Sort's efficiency is particularly notable when compared to simpler algorithms like Bubble Sort and Insertion Sort, which have time complexities of O(n^2). Unlike these quadratic time algorithms, Merge Sort maintains a linearithmic time complexity, making it more suitable for handling substantial amounts of data. Although QuickSort also boasts a similar average time complexity, Merge Sort's stable nature and predictable performance make it preferable in situations where data stability is crucial.

In terms of performance, Merge Sort excels in scenarios where a stable, consistent runtime is essential, but it does come with a higher space complexity due to its use of additional memory during the merging process. Other algorithms like QuickSort may perform slightly better in terms of space efficiency, but Merge Sort's reliability and ease of understanding make it a popular choice in various applications, especially when dealing with large datasets that require efficient, stable sorting.

Quick Sort

Speed
Fast
  • Worst-Case Time Complexity: \(n^2\)
  • Best-Case Time Complexity: \(n * log(n)\)
  • Space Complexity: \(O(log(n))\)
  • Creator: Tom Hoare
  • Made in: 1960

Difficulty
Advanced

QuickSort is a highly efficient and widely used sorting algorithm known for its speed and simplicity. It was developed by Tony Hoare in 1960 while he was working on a machine translation project at the University of Moscow. QuickSort employs a divide-and-conquer strategy, recursively dividing the unsorted list into smaller sublists, sorting them, and then combining them to produce a fully sorted array. The algorithm's efficiency is attributed to its ability to sort in-place, minimizing the need for additional memory.

Tony Hoare's QuickSort is often considered one of the fastest sorting algorithms in practice. Its average time complexity is O(n log n), making it highly efficient for large datasets. QuickSort outperforms Bubble Sort, Insertion Sort, and other O(n^2) algorithms, particularly when dealing with extensive and unsorted data. It also compares favorably to other O(n log n) algorithms like MergeSort. However, the algorithm's worst-case time complexity is O(n^2), which occurs when the pivot selection consistently results in unbalanced partitions. Despite this drawback, QuickSort remains a popular choice due to its general efficiency and widespread use in various programming languages and applications.

In comparison to other sorting algorithms, QuickSort stands out for its average-case performance and adaptability to different datasets. While MergeSort also has an average time complexity of O(n log n), QuickSort often has lower constant factors and performs better in practice. QuickSort's in-place sorting and ability to exploit cache locality contribute to its efficiency, making it a preferred choice in scenarios where speed is crucial.
